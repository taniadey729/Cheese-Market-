**Historical Evolution of the Cheese Industry**

The story of cheese is as old as civilization itself, with archaeological evidence suggesting its production dates back over 7,000 years to ancient Mesopotamia and Egypt, where nomadic herders discovered that milk could curdle into a storable form. Fast-forward through the ages, and cheese-making spread across Europe, becoming a hallmark of regional identities—from the blue-veined Roquefort of France to the holey Emmental of Switzerland. The Industrial Revolution in the 19th century marked a pivotal shift, introducing mechanized processes that scaled production and made cheese accessible beyond artisanal communities. In the United States, the invention of processed cheese by James L. Kraft in 1916 revolutionized the market, paving the way for shelf-stable varieties that fueled the growth of fast food and convenience eating. Post-World War II economic booms further propelled the industry, as refrigeration and global trade networks connected producers with distant consumers. Today, this historical tapestry informs a market that balances heritage craftsmanship with cutting-edge innovation, where small-batch artisanal cheeses coexist with mass-produced blocks, reflecting a blend of nostalgia and progress that keeps the sector dynamically alive.
